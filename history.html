Antique - The Hidden Gem

⚜️ History of Antique

Historians believe that the earliest settlers of Panay Island were tribal Negritos, also known as Atis. According to oral tradition recorded in the Maragtas, ten "datus" or minor tribal Malay chieftains fled persecution in a city called Odtojan in Borneo, ruled by the tyrant Makatunaw. Led by Datu Puti, the datus sailed northward with their families and communities, eventually landing on Panay after departing Borneo. While the historical accuracy of this narrative is debated, it remains an important part of Antique’s cultural heritage.

Upon their arrival, the Bornean datus met the Ati chieftain Datu Marikudo and his wife Maniwantiwan. As a gesture of respect, they presented Datu Marikudo with a salakot (wide-brimmed hat, believed by locals today to be made of pure gold), along with a golden necklace, earrings, bracelets, trinkets, pearls, and fine clothing. In response, Datu Marikudo generously gave the lowlands to the newcomers and moved his Ati tribe to the mountains, which were sacred to them. The legendary arrival of the Bornean boats on Antique’s shores is celebrated each year in the Binirayan Festival.

Following this period, the island of Panay was divided into three sakups: Hantik, Akean, and Irong-Irong. Irong-Irong became modern-day Iloilo, Akean evolved into present-day Aklan and Capiz, and Hantik—also called Hamtik or Hamtic—became Antique. The name "Hantik" was derived from the large black ants found on the island, locally called hantik-hantik.


SPANISH COLONIAL ERA

During the Spanish colonial period, Antique’s coastal location made it vulnerable to raids by Moro pirates. To protect the province and its settlements, Spanish friars and colonial authorities oversaw the construction of a series of watchtowers and fortifications. Notable examples include the Old Watchtower in Libertad and the fortifications on Estaca Hill in Bugasong, which served as early warning posts and defensive positions against seaborne attacks. These structures not only provided protection but also became important landmarks of the province’s colonial history.

In 1790, Antique was officially established as a politico-military province, with the town of Antique (now known as Hamtic) designated as its first capital. Over time, the provincial seat of government was moved to Bugasong (formerly Bugasón) before finally being transferred to San Jose de Buenavista, which remains the provincial capital today.

By the 18th century, Antique had developed into a thriving community, with thousands of families engaged in agriculture, trade, and local crafts. The coastal towns, in particular, became active trading hubs, welcoming merchants from neighboring islands and beyond. During the 19th century, Antique grew further into a prosperous and densely populated area, serving as an important international port. The influx of settlers included Spanish-Filipino and Chinese-Filipino families who contributed to the local economy, commerce, and cultural diversity.

Trade and taxation were key aspects of Antique’s economic life under Spanish rule. Families in various towns paid tribute to the colonial government, and the collection of taxes helped fund both local administration and the broader colonial treasury. The province’s strategic location along the western coast of Panay Island also made it a crucial point for maritime activities, facilitating the movement of goods, people, and cultural influences between Antique and other parts of the Philippines.

Throughout this period, Spanish influence left a lasting mark on Antique’s architecture, religion, and governance. Churches, municipal buildings, and educational institutions were established, while Catholicism became deeply rooted in local communities. At the same time, indigenous customs and local governance structures continued to coexist alongside Spanish systems, creating a unique blend of cultural and social traditions that still influences Antiqueño society today.


JAPANESE OCCUPATION

In 1942, during the Second World War, the Imperial Japanese Army landed in Antique and occupied the province as part of their wider campaign across the Philippines. The occupation brought significant disruption to daily life, as local governance was replaced by military control and resources were redirected to support the Japanese war effort. Many communities faced shortages of food and basic necessities, while strict regulations and surveillance created an atmosphere of fear and uncertainty.

Despite these challenges, resistance quickly emerged among the people of Antique. Local guerrilla groups organized themselves in both coastal and mountainous areas, using their knowledge of the terrain to carry out ambushes and intelligence operations against Japanese forces. These guerrillas played a crucial role in maintaining communication lines, protecting civilians, and weakening enemy control throughout the province.

At the same time, elements of the Philippine Commonwealth Army remained active in Antique. The 6th and 62nd Infantry Divisions, along with units of the Philippine Constabulary, continued their operations despite the occupation. These forces often coordinated with guerrilla fighters, forming a unified resistance movement that strengthened efforts to reclaim the province. Camps and headquarters were established in strategic locations, particularly in upland areas where they were less vulnerable to enemy attacks.

The rugged landscapes of Antique, including its mountains and dense forests, became vital to the resistance. These natural features provided cover for guerrilla bases and allowed fighters to move undetected. Local civilians also contributed significantly by supplying food, shelter, and information, demonstrating strong community solidarity in the face of hardship.

Between 1944 and 1945, the tide of the war began to turn as Allied forces returned to the Philippines. In Antique, combined operations by the Philippine Commonwealth Army, the Philippine Constabulary, and recognized guerrilla units intensified against Japanese troops. Through coordinated attacks and sustained resistance, they successfully drove out occupying forces and liberated the province.

The liberation of Antique marked the end of a difficult chapter in its history. In the years that followed, efforts were made to rebuild communities, restore local governance, and recover from the economic and social impacts of the war. The bravery and resilience shown by the people of Antique during this period remain an important part of the province’s historical identity, remembered through local stories, commemorations, and historical accounts.


PHILIPPINE INDEPENDENCE

🪖 During the Marcos Dictatorship

The early 1970s marked a period of significant turmoil and change in the Philippines, including in the province of Antique. During his campaign to become the first Philippine president to win a second term, Ferdinand Marcos launched a large number of ambitious public works projects funded through foreign loans. While these projects initially aimed to modernize infrastructure, they contributed to a rapid economic decline, culminating in the 1969 balance of payments crisis. This economic downturn led to widespread hardship, rising unemployment, and growing social unrest across the country.

With only a year remaining in his constitutionally limited term, Marcos declared Martial Law in September 1972, effectively extending his rule for fourteen more years. The period of Martial Law is remembered for systematic human rights abuses, including harassment, imprisonment, and violence directed against political opponents, student activists, journalists, religious workers, farmers, and ordinary citizens who spoke out against the regime. The climate of fear and repression deeply affected communities throughout the Philippines, including those in Antique.

One of the most tragic events in Antique during this period was the Bacong Bridge Massacre, which occurred on December 19, 1981, in the town of Culasi. Also referred to as the Culasi incident, it involved the Philippine Constabulary opening fire on protesting farmers at the Bacong River bridge in Barangay Malacañang. Five farmers—Leopoldo A. Anos, Aquilino M. Castillo, Fortunato M. Dalisay, Remegildo P. Dalisay, and Joel B. Plaquino—were killed. These victims were later honored at the Bantayog ng mga Bayani, a memorial that recognizes the heroes and martyrs who fought against the authoritarian regime.

The Bacong Bridge Massacre highlighted the struggles of rural communities during Martial Law, where land disputes, poverty, and demands for social justice often put farmers at odds with government forces. Beyond this tragic event, Antiqueños endured curfews, military patrols, and restrictions on free speech, yet many continued to resist through grassroots organizing, local advocacy, and support for the broader pro-democracy movement.

The period of Martial Law left lasting effects on Antique, shaping its political landscape and community consciousness. Families affected by the repression carried their stories across generations, ensuring that the sacrifices and struggles of those who fought for justice are remembered. Today, these events are commemorated through local history, educational programs, and memorials that honor the resilience and courage of Antiqueños during one of the darkest chapters of Philippine history.

😁 What I think

We all know how brutal the Martial Law era was under Ferdinand Marcos. Thousands of lives were affected—people were arrested, tortured, and silenced simply for speaking out. The economy suffered, civil liberties were restricted, and fear became part of everyday life. That period serves as a stark reminder of what happens when power goes unchecked.

So why does it feel like we are moving backward instead of forward? Why do we see echoes of the past in present-day politics? Why are some people quick to forget, or even ignore, the lessons history has painfully taught us? These questions are not just rhetorical—they challenge us to stay vigilant, informed, and critical. History is not meant to be repeated.

My stand is clear: Marcos and Duterte are no different in terms of authoritarian tendencies and disregard for human rights. We cannot allow history to repeat itself. We must resist efforts to normalize or glorify leaders who threaten democracy, justice, and freedom. No to Sara Duterte 2028. Our country’s future depends on remembering the past, demanding accountability, and protecting the rights and voices of all Filipinos.

Every generation has a responsibility to defend democracy. Forgetting or ignoring the mistakes of the past is a betrayal to those who suffered. Learning from history is the only way to ensure a better, freer, and more just Philippines.
